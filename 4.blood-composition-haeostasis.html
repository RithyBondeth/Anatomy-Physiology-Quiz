Question 1 of 15
What percentage of whole blood is typically composed of plasma?
A 35%
B 45%
C 55%
D 65%
Explanation
Plasma typically makes up about 55% of whole blood, with formed elements (mainly erythrocytes) making up the remaining 45%.
Question 2 of 15
Which of the following is NOT a function of blood?
A Distribution of oxygen and nutrients
B Regulation of pH and temperature
C Production of digestive enzymes
D Protection through clotting and immunity
Explanation
While blood performs distribution, regulation, and protection functions, it does not produce digestive enzymes - that is the role of the digestive system.
Question 3 of 15
Which plasma protein is the main contributor to osmotic pressure?
A Globulins
B Fibrinogen
C Albumin
D Prothrombin
Explanation
Albumin constitutes about 60% of plasma proteins and is the main contributor to osmotic pressure, helping maintain water balance between blood and tissues.
Question 4 of 15
What is the primary function of erythrocytes?
A Immune defense
B Blood clotting
C Gas transport
D Inflammation mediation
Explanation
Erythrocytes (red blood cells) are specialized for gas transport, primarily carrying oxygen from the lungs to tissues and carbon dioxide from tissues to the lungs.
Question 5 of 15
Which leukocyte is primarily responsible for phagocytizing bacteria?
A Eosinophil
B Basophil
C Neutrophil
D Lymphocyte
Explanation
Neutrophils are the most abundant white blood cells and are primarily responsible for phagocytizing bacteria as part of the innate immune response.
Question 6 of 15
What hormone stimulates red blood cell production?
A Insulin
B Erythropoietin
C Thyroxine
D Adrenaline
Explanation
Erythropoietin is a hormone produced mainly by the kidneys that stimulates red bone marrow to produce more red blood cells in response to low oxygen levels.
Question 7 of 15
Which vitamin is essential for stem cell division in erythropoiesis?
A Vitamin A
B Vitamin C
C Vitamin B12
D Vitamin D
Explanation
Vitamin B12 is essential for stem cell division during erythropoiesis. A deficiency can lead to impaired red blood cell production and anemia.
Question 8 of 15
What is the correct order of the three stages of haemostasis?
A Platelet phase → Vascular phase → Coagulation phase
B Coagulation phase → Vascular phase → Platelet phase
C Vascular phase → Platelet phase → Coagulation phase
D Platelet phase → Coagulation phase → Vascular phase
Explanation
The correct order of haemostasis is: 1) Vascular phase (vasoconstriction), 2) Platelet phase (platelet plug formation), 3) Coagulation phase (fibrin clot formation).
Question 9 of 15
During the platelet phase of haemostasis, what do platelets stick to?
A Only to other platelets
B Sticky endothelial surfaces and exposed collagen fibers
C Only to red blood cells
D Only to white blood cells
Explanation
During the platelet phase, platelets adhere to sticky endothelial surfaces and exposed collagen fibers at the site of injury, then aggregate to form a platelet plug.
Question 10 of 15
What is formed during the coagulation phase that traps blood cells and seals the injury?
A Platelet plug
B Fibrin network
C Vascular spasm
D Collagen fibers
Explanation
During the coagulation phase, fibrinogen is converted to fibrin, which forms a mesh network that traps blood cells and platelets, forming a stable clot that seals the injury.
Question 11 of 15
Which of the following is NOT a characteristic of mature erythrocytes?
A They lack a nucleus
B They have organelles for protein synthesis
C They are rich in hemoglobin
D They have a biconcave shape
Explanation
Mature erythrocytes lack a nucleus and organelles, which allows more space for hemoglobin and increases their oxygen-carrying capacity.
Question 12 of 15
Which leukocyte releases histamine and other mediators of inflammation?
A Neutrophil
B Eosinophil
C Basophil
D Monocyte
Explanation
Basophils release histamine and other mediators of inflammation. They also contain heparin, an anticoagulant.
Question 13 of 15
What is the primary stimulus for the release of erythropoietin?
A High blood pressure
B Low blood glucose
C Hypoxia (low blood oxygen)
D Infection
Explanation
Hypoxia (low blood oxygen levels) is the primary stimulus for erythropoietin release, which then stimulates red bone marrow to produce more red blood cells.
Question 14 of 15
Which of the following is a key event during the vascular phase of haemostasis?
A Formation of fibrin network
B Platelet aggregation
C Vasoconstriction
D Conversion of prothrombin to thrombin
Explanation
During the vascular phase, vasoconstriction occurs, which reduces blood flow to the injured area and helps minimize blood loss.
Question 15 of 15
What is the approximate lifespan of a typical erythrocyte?
A 7-10 days
B 30-60 days
C 100-120 days
D 1 year
Explanation
Erythrocytes have a lifespan of approximately 100-120 days, after which they are removed from circulation and broken down, primarily in the spleen.
